# Prompt Flora — Luminária de Mesa

## Contexto para geração

Este prompt é o insumo principal e ponto de partida para toda a estratégia visual da luminária de mesa. Toda geração derivada deve partir deste documento.

---

## Prompt

Design a premium table lamp conceived as a design gift. Price range: R$900–1800. Small-batch or made-to-order production with Brazilian suppliers.

### The user

Beatriz, 43, architect. Owns a 110m² apartment in Noroeste, Brasília's newest planned neighborhood — built from 2012, high-rise contemporary architecture, open floor plans, generous ceiling heights, high-end finishes. She renovated the apartment herself. It is her permanent laboratory.

She buys few objects but buys with conviction. Prefers less and better. A cheap object replaced in two years costs more than an expensive one kept for twenty. She tests material honesty before any aesthetic judgment: laminate imitating wood, painted plastic imitating metal — identified in seconds, disqualified permanently. The simulation offends her more than the cheap material would on its own.

She knows Artemide, Anglepoise, Flos, Lumini without them needing to announce themselves. Owns two Lumini lamps at her office, a Zanine Caldas chair reproduced by a trusted woodworker, reads design publications as pleasure not obligation. Plans vacations around cities with relevant architecture.

Her defining phrase: "I don't buy objects. I choose company for a space."

The lamp would likely live on her bedside table (left side, where she reads), or on her home office desk, or on the living room console. In all cases it shares the surface with other curated objects: books, plants, collected pieces. It must work within that composition without subordinating or dominating.

### The gift context

Buyer and user are different people with different evaluation criteria. The buyer decides at point of sale or catalog based on appearance, packaging, and reputation. The user decides over time based on performance, comfort, and spatial coherence.

The first person to handle this object may not be Beatriz. The buyer may not know freijó, may not recognize the level of finishing, may not have the technical repertoire to justify the price. The unboxing experience, the material context text, and the presentation surface are part of the design for the same reason as form and finish: perceived value begins before the object reaches the hands of the person who will use it.

A small box with a small lamp inside may not convey the symbolic weight that a R$1,200 gift needs at the moment of delivery.

### Competitive territory

The lamp exists in a market with a specific structure:

**Nordic contemporary** (Norm Architects/Hashira, Louis Poulsen/Panthella): own refinement, portability, and established brand reputation. Deliver linen, ash wood, and considered proportions. Cannot claim Brazilian territory — this is a structural, irreversible asymmetry. The Hashira is the most direct threat: artisanal materiality, solid brand, portability, competitive price.

**Italian historical** (Artemide/Nessino, Flos): own iconic authorship and design-history capital. Deliver plastic at touch. The name sells before the box opens; the material disappoints the moment Beatriz holds the object. "Classic Italian design" requires no explanation when buying — but the ABS is noticed in the second it's touched.

**French-star authorship** (Kartell/Bon Jour Unplugged by Starck): portability plus celebrity name. Plastic body and PMMA diffuser. "It's Starck" is a complete purchase decision — but materially hollow for this user.

**Brazilian authorship** (Fernando Prado/LED'S PLAY T): real wood, national origin, traceable authorship. Fails in sculptural presence — 15cm height disappears on a composed surface. The identity is there; the legibility as premium gift is not fully resolved.

**Independent Brazilian makers**: growing segment of studios working with natural materials (wood, ceramic, leather, rattan), R$700–2000, distributed via Instagram and design fairs. Closest in language and process; least consolidated in brand and reach.

### Five unresolved gaps (the territory this lamp must occupy)

1. **Material honesty × Sculptural presence.** No existing product resolves both simultaneously. Those with real materials are too small; those with formal presence use materials that fail at touch.

2. **Brazilian authorial identity × Immediate premium gift legibility.** No existing product is both unmistakably Brazilian and immediately legible as high-value gift without explanation.

3. **Alive when ON × Silent when OFF.** The lamp exists 14 hours/day without emitting light. It must have formal reason to exist when off — not look like a dormant tool. When on, light must be precise, warm (2700-3000K), adjustable without interrupting workflow.

4. **Professional technical scrutiny × Lasting affective bond.** Must pass an architect's structural eye (every joint, every material transition) AND create emotional attachment that prevents replacement at next space update.

5. **Material that gains life with use × Formal language that doesn't age as dated citation.** Surface that patinas, registers touch, changes with time without degrading. Form with no ornament belonging to a specific era — reduced to principles: proportion, rhythm, balance.

### Material and construction requirements

- Primary material must be named before any form exploration — its constructive logic guides joints, finish, and expected aging
- No material simulating another material
- Every material must age predictably over 10+ years
- If wood: real, identifiable Brazilian species. If glass: visibly handmade. If metal: honest about what it is
- Electrical components must meet Brazilian standards (ABNT NBR 5410, Inmetro certified)
- Cable minimum 1.8m, Brazilian plug standard (NBR 14136) — cable quality must match the object (no generic white wire)
- LED source, 2700-3000K, dimmable preferred
- Stable base on flat surface, no mounting required
- Weight compatible with manual transport and frequent repositioning
- Production viable with national suppliers

### Brasília as design context

Brasília is a UNESCO Creative City of Design (2017). It was founded as a project — Lucio Costa and Oscar Niemeyer embedded in the city a vocabulary of rigorous geometry and formal clarity that shaped generations of creative professionals. The user lives inside this vocabulary daily.

But the lamp lives in Noroeste and Samambaia, not in the Plano Piloto monumental axis. It lives in the contemporary Brasília — young neighborhoods still constructing their visual identity. The product should dialogue with this geometric heritage without citing it literally. No columns, no curves referencing palaces, no pastiche of modernist icons.

### What the lamp must be

- Formally self-sufficient when off (sculptural presence through geometry, proportion, material weight)
- Materially honest at every point of contact (what you see is what you touch)
- Culturally Brazilian without being folkloric (origin readable by those with repertoire, beautiful to those without)
- Buildable in small batches with Brazilian suppliers and processes
- Sized for presence on a composed surface (not disappearing at 15cm, not dominating at 50cm)
- Detailed with visible constructive integrity at every joint and material transition
- Containing an element of singularity that doesn't replicate industrially (natural wood variation, series numbering, unique formal decision)
- Capable of creating lasting affective bond through material aging and physical interaction over years

### What the lamp must NOT be

- A literal representation of anything (no flowers, no architectural miniatures, no figurative elements)
- Generic enough to belong to any design course project anywhere
- Dependent on the light being on to justify its existence
- Made of material that pretends to be something else
- So precious it can't be touched daily
- So minimal it has no presence
- Nordic refinement with a Brazilian label
- Artisanal rusticity cosplaying as premium
- A product that requires explanation to communicate value — the material speaks first, then the form, then the story

### Output direction

Generate visual concepts that resolve the five gaps simultaneously. Prioritize: material close-ups showing grain/texture/joint quality, the lamp OFF in a composed environment, the silhouette against Brasília's late-afternoon golden light, and the moment of first touch (hand approaching the object, revealing scale and surface).
